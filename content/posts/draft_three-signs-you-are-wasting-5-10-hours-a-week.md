# Three signs your business is wasting 5-10 hours a week

If you run a small shop in Olean or anywhere in Western New York, you've probably said one of these three things this week. Maybe out loud, maybe just under your breath while you watched somebody on your team retype the same thing for the fourth time.

I'm going to tell you what each one looks like in practice — what's actually happening when an owner tells me one of these — and how I'd start fixing it. None of this requires new software. Most of it uses tools you already pay for.

## Sign 1: "I'm answering the same question over and over."

If you or someone on your team is answering the same five questions every day, that's the easiest pattern to spot and the cheapest one to fix.

What it looks like: a customer calls or emails to ask what time you open, what your prices are, whether you do a specific service, whether you're booking out three weeks or six. The answer is on your website — but they didn't read the website, they called. Multiply that by twenty calls a day, three minutes each, and that's an hour of your day on questions you've already answered in writing somewhere.

The fix is rarely a chatbot. It's usually three things, in this order:

1. Write the five questions and answers down in one place — a Google Doc, a Word file, doesn't matter — so the answers are consistent regardless of who picks up the phone.
2. Make a one-page printable that lives on the front desk and gets handed to walk-ins.
3. Set up an Outlook or Gmail reply template that pastes the relevant answer into a draft email in two clicks.

Three to five hours a week back, no new vendor, total setup time about an hour. If you want to layer an AI tool on top later — something that drafts the reply for you using your own Q&A doc as the source — that's a half-hour add-on. Not a project.

## Sign 2: "Someone's retyping the same data into a different system."

This one hides in plain sight. You probably don't see it happen because the person doing it has stopped complaining about it. They just do it every Monday at 9 AM and it eats forty minutes.

What it looks like: an order comes in through one system (your website, a booking app, an email) and someone copies the customer's name, phone, address, and what they ordered into a second system (your accounting software, your CRM, a spreadsheet). Same data, two systems, one human bridge in the middle. If you have three systems that need the same data, that human is now in the middle of three pipes.

This pattern almost never needs a new tool — it needs a connector between tools you already pay for. Microsoft 365 has Power Automate, which a non-technical person can configure in an afternoon. Google Workspace has the same thing built into their app integrations. Zapier costs $20 a month and connects almost anything to almost anything else.

The setup pays for itself the first week. The hours saved compound forever afterward, because you're never paying that re-typing tax again. If your business has been running for more than two years, there's a 90% chance there's at least one of these pipes hiding in someone's workflow. They've usually stopped mentioning it.

## Sign 3: "We forgot to follow up — again."

This one is the most expensive of the three because it's not just lost time, it's lost revenue.

What it looks like: a quote went out three weeks ago and you haven't heard back. A customer left a voicemail and somebody was supposed to call them back. A repair was finished and the customer never came to pick it up. Inspection reminders, license-renewal reminders, the seasonal thing you've always meant to do but the year is half-gone now.

These are dropped balls. Every business has them. The owners I talk to assume the fix is "we need to be more disciplined." It's not. The fix is structural: the system that produces the lead, quote, or job should also produce the follow-up reminder, automatically, with no human remembering required.

For a small shop, this usually means one of three things:
- Calendar tasks set to auto-create the reminder when an event happens (Microsoft Copilot or Google Workspace can do this).
- An email scheduled-send so the follow-up email writes itself the moment the quote ships, then sits in a drafts queue for one human review before it goes out.
- A simple text-message cadence (NY State inspection reminders are the classic example — three texts and one email over two weeks usually pulls back a customer who would have drifted to the chain shop).

What you save in time is real (a couple hours a week of "did I follow up on…" anxiety alone). What you save in revenue is bigger and harder to count — but if even one out of every twenty dropped customers comes back, the math works out fast.

## What this adds up to

If any one of those three signs is happening in your business, you're probably losing five hours a week to it. If two of them are happening, you're well past that.

The audits I run start with the same conversation: walk me through how a typical week runs, where the boring repeated stuff lives, what your team complains about. The point isn't to add another vendor. The point is to find the places where AI tools — the practical kind, not the buzzword kind — can eliminate 5-10 hours of repetitive work every week, using the tools you already pay for.
